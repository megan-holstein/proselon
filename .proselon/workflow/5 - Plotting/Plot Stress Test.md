# Plot Stress Test

Tier: craft — adversarial story judgment; run with the strongest available model.

Rubric assessors work *through a checklist*: they confirm what's present. Adversaries *hunt*: they discover what's wrong. This pass exists because the most common reader complaints about plot — "the villain was an idiot," "why didn't she just tell him," "the twist made no sense in hindsight," "the middle third did nothing" — are exactly what no cooperative checklist surfaces, and a hole found after thirty drafted scenes costs either a rewrite or a patch in prose. Three attackers, each in its own fresh context, each trying to break the plot from a different direction. All three are **findings-only**: they report; they change nothing.

## When It Runs

- **In full**, after the book plot clears its assessment panel (5.2) and before chapter mapping (5.4) — attack the plot while it's still cheap to change.
- **Scoped**, after the chapter map clears its panel (5.6): re-run the attackers against what the map *added* — reveal ordering, chapter-level causality, new connective tissue — not against what 5.2 already stress-tested.

The attack is bounded: one full attack, findings routed, then one verification pass on the revised plot confirming the named holes are closed. Not an open loop.

## The Three Attackers

Run all three in parallel, in separate fresh contexts. None sees the others' reports.

### The Antagonist Advocate

**Loads:** the book plot, `Plot/Series Plot.md`, the antagonist's (and any major opposing faction's) character/faction profiles, relevant worldbuilding.

Play the antagonist. Take their stated goals, knowledge, resources, competence, and allies at full face value — everything canon says they are — and win. For every phase of the plot, ask: given what this character knows and wants *right now*, what is their best move? Then check it against what the plot has them do.

Report every finding of the form:
- **The winning move the plot ignores** — a path to the antagonist's goal that nothing in canon forecloses.
- **The convenient stupidity** — a moment the antagonist acts below their established competence so the protagonist's plan can work.
- **The idle stretch** — chapters where the antagonist waits, off-page, for the protagonist to catch up, with no in-world reason to wait.

For each: what the antagonist would actually do, and what the plot must add (a constraint, a cost, a piece of missing knowledge — established *before* the moment it's needed) for the plotted behavior to hold.

### The Plot-Hole Hunter

**Loads:** the book plot, character profiles for every major character, relevant worldbuilding, `Plot/Specs.md`.

Enumerate, beat by beat:
- **Out-of-character convenience** — anyone acting against their established competence, knowledge, or motivation to keep the plot on rails. The idiot-plot test: does the story survive everyone being as smart as their profiles claim?
- **The unsent message** — conflicts that dissolve if two characters have one honest conversation, with no established reason they can't or wouldn't.
- **Load-bearing coincidence** — chance events the causal chain cannot stand without. One coincidence that *creates* trouble is genre-legal; a coincidence that *resolves* trouble is a hole.
- **Reveals that don't survive a second read** — for each twist, replay earlier beats as if the truth were known. Does everyone's prior behavior still make sense? A twist that retroactively breaks earlier scenes is a hole with a delay on it.
- **Timeline and logistics** — travel, healing, information transit, and resource math that doesn't work.

### The Kill-Your-Darlings Editor

**Loads:** the book plot (plus the chapter map, on the scoped rerun), `Plot/Specs.md`.

For every subplot, POV thread, and named story element, argue the case for **cutting it**: what would the book lose? Write the one-sentence defense. Anything without a real defense — it advances no thread, deepens no one, pays nothing off — is flab that the sagging-middle criterion can't see, because it's *doing something*, just not enough to earn its pages. Rank the weakest three candidates for cutting or merging, with what each cut would free up.

## Routing Findings

The orchestrating agent merges the three reports and routes like assessment findings: self-correctable holes go through the revision loop; anything structural, cascading, or hinging on a creative choice (cutting a subplot, changing what the antagonist knows) surfaces to the author with a recommendation. If the findings are pervasive — the plot only works because several people are convenient — treat it as a regeneration case, not a patch list. Findings that indict the *series plot* escalate upstream.
