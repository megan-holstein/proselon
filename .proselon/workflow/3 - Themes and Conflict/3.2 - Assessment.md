# Themes and Conflict Assessment

Evaluate the Themes and Conflict document for craft quality before downstream work commits to it. Themes are the spine of the story — if they're vague, didactic, or disconnected from character, the cascade will produce literary-essay-flavored novels rather than emotionally alive ones.

## Prerequisites

Read these files before assessing:

1. `Plot/Themes and Conflict.md` — the document being assessed
2. `Plot/Series Plot.md` — for the established story arc the themes must serve *(if present)*
3. `Plot/Specs.md` — for genre conventions (different genres have different thematic registers) *(if present)*

Assess against what exists at this point in the workflow. A missing file marked *(if present)* is not a weakness; skip the criteria that depend on it.

## Checklist

### Theme Specificity

Each theme is named and phrased as a question or tension, not a thesis. "What does loyalty cost?" works; "Loyalty is costly" does not. The former invites dramatization; the latter invites lecture.

### Project-Specific Justification

Each theme has a stated reason it belongs in *this* project, not a generic literary aspiration. A theme included because it's "important" without project-specific traction is decorative.

### Reader Landing

For each theme, the document names the felt understanding the reader walks away with. Vague endings ("the reader understands the complexity") suggest the theme hasn't been thought through to its payoff.

### Inner Conflict Realness

Each major character has a real psychological tension — competing desires, beliefs, or instincts in active conflict. Generic flaws ("she's stubborn") or single drives ("he wants revenge") don't qualify. The tension must be internal; a character versus the external world is plot, not inner conflict.

### Inner Conflict Specificity

Each character's tension is specific enough to dramatize. "Loves her sister but resents her" is dramatizable. "Struggles with identity" is not.

### Theme-Character Integration

Each inner conflict connects to at least one theme. If a character's psychology has no thematic resonance, either the character serves only plot mechanics or the themes don't yet cover the territory the characters actually occupy.

### Outer Conflict Coherence

The central external conflict is specific (a situation, antagonist, or pressure — not a vague "they must survive"). The stakes escalation pattern is articulated, not assumed.

### Arc Shape

Each theme has a defined arc across the series (or acts, for a standalone). Themes that appear in Book 1 and Book 3 without articulation of how Book 2 develops them have a sagging middle in their meaning, not just their plot.

Each character's inner arc has shape: starting state, pressure points, breaking point, ending state. Inner arcs that go straight from "starting state" to "ending state" without pressure or breaking points are too clean.

### Thematic Diversity

The themes are not facets of one larger theme in disguise. If "betrayal," "trust," and "loyalty" are all listed as separate themes, they're really one theme with three labels. Genuinely separate themes ask genuinely different questions.

### Thematic Distinctiveness

At least one theme — or the angle of attack on a familiar theme — is specific to this premise rather than importable into any comp title in `Plot/Specs.md`. Test against the comp list: if every theme could be pasted unchanged into every comp, the slate is stock. Familiar themes are not the problem — "what does loyalty cost?" has powered a thousand good books — but the *pairing* of theme and premise should produce an angle only this project can take. Name what this premise lets the theme do that the comps can't. *(Skip if `Plot/Specs.md` is absent.)*

### Avoidance of Didacticism

The document presents themes as questions and tensions, not as lessons to teach the reader. Watch for theme statements like "this story shows that...", "the reader will learn...", or "the message is..." — these signal the writing will be preachy unless the framing changes.

### Genre Fit

The thematic register matches genre expectations from `Plot/Specs.md`. Literary fiction can hold ambiguity and contradiction; commercial genre fiction usually wants clearer thematic resolution. A mismatch between thematic complexity and genre signals isn't necessarily wrong, but it should be a deliberate choice.

### Symbol and Motif Discipline

If symbols or motifs are listed, each one signals a specific theme and has a stated evolution. Generic "water represents change" placeholders without project-specific deployment are decorative.

## Output Format

```
## Themes and Conflict Assessment

**Strengths** (ranked)
1. ...

**Weaknesses** (ranked by severity)
1. ...

**Required Revisions**
- [Only include if verdict is Needs revision. Each item should be specific enough to edit the document.]

**Verdict**: Approved / Needs revision
```

Concise, ranked, actionable. If the verdict is Needs revision, weaknesses must include specific enough detail to act on.

Use Needs revision only for issues that would corrupt downstream work — thesis-shaped or didactic themes, inner conflicts too vague to dramatize, themes disconnected from the characters who must carry them, or a theme slate importable unchanged into any comp (a Thematic Distinctiveness failure — a stock slate corrupts downstream work as surely as a vague one, because every later document inherits its genericness). Do not require every symbol, motif, or minor character to be fully worked out before approval.

When the failing criterion is Thematic Distinctiveness, the Required Revision should direct generating **alternative theme slates** to compare against, not rewording the current one — a stock theme rephrased is a stock theme.
