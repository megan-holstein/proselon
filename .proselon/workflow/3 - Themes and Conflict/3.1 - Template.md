# Themes and Conflict Template

Use this template to define the project's themes, the central outer conflict, and each major character's inner conflict — and to map how those forces arc across the series. This is the spine of the story's meaning. Plot is what happens; this document is what the happening is *for*.

Themes drive everything downstream. Voice serves themes (a wry voice and a lyrical voice deliver different thematic registers). Book plots deploy themes. Chapter and scene plots pressure character inner conflicts. Drafting renders all of it.

## Interview Goals

Before filling the template, gather enough information to answer:

- What is this story *about* — not what happens, but what it's exploring? What human question does it press on?
- What does the reader feel they've understood by the end?
- What is the central external conflict? What are the antagonistic forces or systems?
- For each major character: what internal contradiction are they caught in? What competing desires, beliefs, or instincts are at war?
- How does each character's inner conflict connect to a theme?
- How do themes escalate across books — what gets harder, more demanding, less resolvable?
- Which themes resolve and which remain permanently open?

Do not draft plot beats or scene material in this step. This document defines forces; later steps deploy them.

## Output Template

```markdown
---
type: Themes & Conflict
title: Themes and Conflict
description: [one-line summary of what the story is about]
---
# Themes and Conflict

## Themes

For each major theme:

### [Theme Name]

- **Core question**: [the human question this theme is asking — phrase as a question or tension, not a thesis]
- **Why it matters here**: [why this theme belongs in this project specifically, not just generally]
- **Where the reader lands**: [the felt understanding the reader walks away with — what's clarified, what's complicated, what remains]

Repeat for each major theme. Projects have 2–4 active themes; go to 5 only with explicit justification — more usually means the project is unfocused or some themes are facets of others.

## Outer Conflict

- **Central external conflict**: [the primary external situation, problem, or pressure the story is built around]
- **Antagonistic forces or systems**: [what or who creates the pressure — could be a character, an institution, a structure, a circumstance]
- **Stakes escalation pattern**: [how the external pressure intensifies across the series — what gets harder, riskier, more impossible to ignore]

## Inner Conflict

For each major character (POV characters and other characters with arcs):

### [Character Name]

- **Core psychological tension**: [the competing desires, beliefs, or instincts the character is caught between — phrase as "X versus Y" or "wants X but believes Y"]
- **Connection to theme(s)**: [which theme(s) this character's inner work serves or embodies]
- **Inner arc across the series**:
  - **Starting state**: [what the character believes, wants, fears, or relies on at the beginning]
  - **Pressure points**: [the events or relationships that force the tension to surface]
  - **Breaking point**: [the moment the character can no longer hold the contradiction]
  - **Ending state**: [resolved, transformed, partially open, or permanently fractured]

Repeat for each major character.

## Thematic Arc Across Books

For each theme:

### [Theme Name]

- **Book 1**: [how this theme is introduced and first pressured]
- **Book N**: [one line per planned book — middle books escalate, complicate, or shift; the final book resolves, reframes, or remains intentionally open]

If the project is a standalone, structure as Act 1 / Act 2 / Act 3 instead.

## Theme-to-Character Mapping

A summary table connecting themes to the characters who carry them most:

| Theme | Primary carrier | Secondary carriers | How it manifests |
|-------|-----------------|--------------------| -----------------|
| [theme] | [character] | [characters] | [how the theme shows up in their behavior, choices, or arc] |

## Theme-to-Plot Mapping

For each theme, identify the plot events that pressure it most:

- **[Theme]**: [list of major plot events or sequences where this theme is forced into the open]

## Symbols, Motifs, and Recurring Images

Optional but valuable. If the project uses symbols, motifs, or recurring sensory images to signal themes, document them here:

- **[Symbol or image]**: [what theme it signals, where it recurs, how it evolves]

## Open Questions

- Unresolved thematic decisions
- Inner conflicts not yet pinned down for specific characters
- Tensions between themes that need to be reconciled or kept productive
```

## Completion Criteria

A usable Themes and Conflict document:

- Names 2–4 active themes (5 only with explicit justification), each phrased as a question or tension (not a thesis statement).
- Each theme has a stated reason it belongs in *this* project.
- Defines the central outer conflict with specific antagonistic forces.
- Gives each major character a real psychological tension — competing things they want, believe, or fear — not generic flaws.
- Connects each inner conflict to at least one theme.
- Maps how themes escalate across books (or across acts for a standalone).
- Connects themes to specific characters and specific plot events.
- Records unresolved decisions in `Open Questions` rather than hiding them.
- Avoids didacticism — themes are presented as questions or tensions, not lessons the reader is supposed to learn.
