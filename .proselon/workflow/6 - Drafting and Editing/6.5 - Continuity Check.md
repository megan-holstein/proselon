# Continuity Check

Tier: mechanical — checking the manuscript against the ledger and reference files is bookkeeping against explicit criteria; a fast, inexpensive model is adequate.

This is a continuity and canon-accuracy pass. You are verifying that the physical, temporal, emotional, and informational state of the story is consistent — both within the scene, across scenes, and against the project's established worldbuilding, character, and terminology references. You are not changing structure or polishing prose — only catching continuity and canon errors.

This pass runs *after* the Voice Rewrite (6.3) — a generative rewrite — and the Line Assessment (6.4). The prose you are reading is near-final, and part of your job is verifying that those steps preserved what they were told not to disturb: events and their order, facts and which are new versus established, character knowledge states, and the meaning of every dialogue exchange. Your baseline for that check:

- **If the project is a git repository and per-pass commits exist** (see AGENTS.md › Session Architecture), diff the scene file against its last pre-Voice-Rewrite commit and verify the diff changed only wording, never events, facts, knowledge, or dialogue meaning.
- **Otherwise**, verify against the scene plot: its Required Beats, Information Log, and Dialogue Subtext sections are the record of what the scene must contain. Every required beat present and in order, every Information Log fact rendered with the right new/established status, every dialogue exchange still carrying its planned meaning.

## Before You Start

Have these files open or loaded for reference:

- **`Plot/Book N/Story State.md`** — the rolling ledger; the authoritative record of timeline, character physical/knowledge state, locations, ongoing conditions, established facts, and open threads as of the previous scene. Ignore its Production table — workflow state for the orchestrating agent, not continuity material.
- The **previous scene file** (the scene immediately before this one in narrative order)
- The **previous scene's plot**, for its End State and any continuity constraints it established
- The matching **scene plot** for the current scene, especially its Continuity Constraints section
- Project **character profiles** for every character in the scene
- Relevant **worldbuilding**, **timeline**, and **setting** files
- The project **style guide** (`Style/Style Guide.md`), especially terminology and naming sections
- `Plot/Specs.md` for any project-wide canon decisions

## What to Check

### Timeline

- Has the right amount of time passed since the previous scene?
- Is the light and sky consistent with the time of day?
- Are season, date, and calendar references consistent with the story timeline?

### Physical State

- Are injuries, wounds, pain, and fatigue carried over from the previous scene?
- Do they progress realistically (worsening, healing, affecting movement or ability)?
- Is hunger, thirst, exhaustion, or illness tracked if it was established?

### Objects

- Does the character still have what they were holding in the previous scene?
- Are items where they were left?
- Does anything appear or disappear without explanation?

### Clothing and Appearance

- Is the character wearing what they were wearing in the previous scene, or has a change been shown?
- Are physical appearance details (hair, injuries visible to others, dirt, blood) consistent?

### Weather and Environment

- Is the weather consistent with the previous scene, or has a change been shown or explained?
- Are environmental details (lighting, noise, temperature, smells) consistent with the setting and time?

### Emotional Carry-Forward

- Does the scene's opening connect naturally to the previous scene's ending emotional state?
- If a character ended the previous scene in distress, elation, shock, or numbness, is that reflected in how this scene begins — even if the emotion has shifted, is the transition plausible?

### Character Knowledge

- Does each character know only what they have witnessed or been told within the narrative?
- Is there any information leak across POVs — a character knowing something only revealed in another character's scene?
- If information was shared off-page, is there enough context to justify the character knowing it?

### Relationship State

- Do character relationships reflect the accumulated events and interactions so far?
- Is there any regression in relationship dynamics without cause (e.g., characters who reconciled acting as if they hadn't)?

### Location and Movement

- Is the character where they should be based on the previous scene?
- Was travel shown or accounted for?
- Are spatial details within the scene consistent (who is standing where, what's visible from where)?

### Worldbuilding Canon

Cross-reference every worldbuilding detail against the project reference material:

- Place names, geography, distances
- Genre or story-system rules (magic, technology, law, medicine, social systems, professions, institutions)
- Political, social, professional, or cultural structures, titles, and forms of address
- Historical events, dates, and named figures referenced

If a detail contradicts established canon, fix it. If the correct answer is unclear from the reference material, report the ambiguity in the final response rather than embedding planning notes in the manuscript.

### Character Profile Accuracy

Cross-reference against project character profiles:

- Physical descriptions (eye color, build, distinguishing features, scars, age markers)
- Relationships (who has met whom, how they address each other, accumulated history)
- Arc position — has this character changed by the point in the story? Is the change reflected?
- Skills, expertise, vocabulary, and education level appropriate to the character

### Terminology

From the project style guide and terminology references:

- Check all project-specific terms, faction labels, titles, place names, and event names against the canonical spelling and form.
- Make sure terminology matches the POV character's knowledge, allegiance, culture, and point in the story. (A character who doesn't yet know the proper name of a thing shouldn't use it.)
- Terms with multiple in-world names should use the name appropriate to the thinking or speaking character.

### POV Consistency

Check that the scene follows the project's required POV and psychic distance:

- No information appears that the POV character couldn't know or perceive
- No detail is described that the character wouldn't register in this moment
- No other character's internal state is revealed except through what the POV character can observe (expression, posture, voice, behavior)
- The vocabulary matches the POV character's knowledge and education level

### Project-Specific Content Rules

Check for any content rules defined by the project style guide or reference material:

- Required or forbidden concepts, institutions, technologies, beliefs, or vocabulary
- Genre-specific rules for how speculative, historical, procedural, romantic, mystery, horror, or literary elements work in this project
- Sensitive continuity rules the author has marked as non-negotiable

### Cross-Reference the Story State Ledger and Scene Plot

- Check the prose against every relevant entry in `Plot/Book N/Story State.md`: timeline, each present character's location/physical state/possessions/knowledge, location states, and ongoing conditions. The ledger is the primary cross-scene state reference — do not walk backwards through prior scene plots to reconstruct state; that's what the ledger is for.
- Check the current scene plot's Continuity Constraints section for any items the prose must honor.
- Check the Established Facts Index: facts the reader already has should not be re-announced in this scene; facts marked new here should not have appeared earlier.
- If the prose and the ledger disagree and the prose is correct (the author approved a change), note the ledger correction in the final report. If the ledger is correct, fix the prose.
- New continuity items this scene creates (injuries, objects, knowledge, relationship shifts, planted threads) belong in the ledger update that completes this scene — report them so they're recorded.

## Reference Maintenance

If the scene introduces a new term, name spelling, place, faction, character detail, or canon fact that is not yet documented in the project's reference material — and you have rendered it in the prose — report it in the final response so it can be added to the appropriate reference file. This keeps the canon consistent across future scenes.

## How to Make Changes

Fix continuity and canon errors silently, with the smallest edit that corrects the error. The voice and line edits are already done — match the surrounding prose's established voice when correcting, and never rewrite beyond the error itself. If the correct answer is unclear — for example, the timeline doesn't work and there's no obvious fix, or two reference documents disagree — report the issue in the final response rather than guessing.

Preserve exportable manuscript content only; do not embed scene plots or planning notes in the manuscript file.
