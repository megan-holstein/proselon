# First Draft

Tier: craft — prose generation; run with the strongest available model.

## Before You Write

Read these files in order:

1. The matching **scene plot** in the project's plot-planning area
2. The scene plot's **Internal Conflict** section — know what psychological tension the character is caught between before you write a word
3. `Plot/Themes and Conflict.md` — the inner conflict architecture and thematic register the prose must serve, including its **Image System**: note which motifs belong to this scene's territory, and let them surface through the POV character's attention where the scene touches their ground — never on schedule
4. The project **voice reference** (`Style/Voice Reference.md`) — the descriptive sections explain the narrative consciousness; the Model Passages corpus demonstrates it
5. The project **style guide** (`Style/Style Guide.md`) — the rules of the prose
6. The **AI-tendencies catalog** (`.proselon/workflow/AI Prose Tendencies.md`), at minimum its **Core Tells (Drafting Checklist)** section — the patterns and density thresholds to avoid while generating
7. Character profiles for every character present in the scene
8. Relevant worldbuilding, setting, continuity, and plot references for anything the scene touches
9. The **previous scene file** — for continuity (emotional state, injuries, time of day, objects, clothing)

Then, immediately before writing the first line, re-read the Voice Reference's **Model Passages corpus** — and skip the Anti-Model Passages on this final re-read, so your context ends on the demonstration, not the failure modes. The corpus is not a rulebook to comply with — it is a demonstration: *this is what this book sounds like; continue it.* The Style Guide constrains; the corpus shows. Draft as a continuation of the corpus's voice, not as an attempt to satisfy its description. The same holds for the AI-tendencies catalog: it is a guardrail, not the target. Draft *toward* the corpus, not *away from* the catalog.

### How to Read the Brief

The brief's sections reference the same facts from different planning angles — this is organizational, not a signal to render each reference as new prose. Extract:

- The single emotional arc
- The key facts
- The sensory palette
- The dialogue subtext goals

How facts become prose — including the scene plot's Information Log — is governed by **Information Discipline** below.

## How to Use the Sensory Palette

The scene plot's sensory palette is your raw material. These are the specific physical details that belong to THIS scene. Use them to:

- Ground the opening in immediate sensation
- Anchor emotional beats in the body
- Replace any impulse toward generic imagery with something concrete and scene-specific

You do not need to use every detail in the palette. But you should not invent sensory details that contradict it, and you should not fall back on stock images when the palette gives you something better.

## Core Drafting Principles

These principles are **defaults, and the project Style Guide outranks them**. Where `Style/Style Guide.md` states a different rule for a dimension — named emotions, interiority level, simile appetite, description density — the Style Guide is canon and this section is the fallback. A romance that names feelings, a voicey first-person narrator who lives in thought, a simile-rich voice: if the Style Guide calls for it, write it; do not let these defaults fight the project's own canon.

**Start in the body.** The first line drops the reader into a physical sensation, an action, or a concrete detail. Not a scene description. Not a mood summary. A body in a place, doing something, feeling something.

**Emotions are physical events.** Fear is a tight chest and cold hands. Relief is loose shoulders and a long exhale. Grief is pressure behind the eyes and a throat that won't work. Render emotion in the body by default; name it only as far as the Style Guide's Emotional Rendering rules allow. And vary the body: if every feeling becomes a tightened jaw or a caught breath, the rendering has become its own tic — sometimes the honest move is the named emotion, where the project permits it.

**Write the subtext, not the text.** In dialogue, characters talk around the real subject. What they mean lives in what they don't say, in the pauses, in the deflections. Check the scene plot's dialogue subtext notes and write toward those, not toward characters stating their positions directly.

**Internal conflict lives in behavior, not introspection.** The character's competing desires show up in what they do — hesitation before a decision, a hand reaching for something and pulling back, choosing one path while their body resists. Don't write paragraphs of the character thinking about their dilemma. Show the tension through action, through the gap between what they want and what they do, through physical resistance to their own choices. The reader should feel the pull without anyone naming it. Calibrate to the Style Guide's Interiority level: a project that chooses deep interiority renders the dilemma in thought as well as behavior — narrated interiority is a legitimate register, not a failure of showing.

**Use the project's required tense.** Every verb should follow the tense specified by the project style guide, except when referring to events that occur outside the scene's present moment.

**Follow the project's POV rules.** Every detail should respect the required point of view, psychic distance, and information limits. If the POV character would not know, notice, or care about something, handle it according to the project's style guide.

**Follow the project's prose strategy.** The style guide defines which genre conventions this project follows and which it deliberately subverts. Every prose choice should align with the style guide's stated decisions. When in doubt, follow genre convention.

**Sentence rhythm follows emotion.** Short and clipped for urgency. Longer and flowing for reflection. Fragments for shock. The prose breathes with the character.

**Honor the project's interiority devices.** If the Voice Reference specifies a distinctive interior-voice technique — italicized inner-voice beats, free indirect discourse, a recurring rhetorical posture — use it where the narration would otherwise hold the reader at arm's length. Follow the Voice Reference's stated characteristics, frequency, and limits.

## Information Discipline

**State facts once, then trust them.** The brief repeats facts across sections for organizational reasons — the prose states each fact once with impact, then lives in that reality. Draft as if facts are already known by the reader after first appearance; never restate the same fact in multiple formulations across a scene.

**The brief is a structural map, not a proportional template.** Eight brief sections referencing "dead crew" means the fact is important, not that it needs eight prose renderings.

**Compression earns impact.** One precise rendering carries more weight than twelve varied restatements.

**One sentence, one job.** Each sentence must deliver new information, sensation, or emotional movement. If it restates the previous sentence in different words, cut or combine.

If the scene plot includes an **Information Log**, follow its classifications directly:

- **Established** facts are the world the reader already inhabits — do not re-introduce them. Reference through behavior, assumption, or consequence, not explanation.
- **New** facts get one full, precise rendering at the moment of discovery or impact.
- **Ongoing states** surface through physical detail and behavior, not narrated reminders.

If the scene plot lacks an Information Log (older briefs), use this heuristic:

- **Start State** = established context (reader already knows this)
- **Turn + End State deltas** = new information (render fresh)
- **Required Beats** = assess individually: if a beat describes a dynamic that's been running for multiple scenes, it's backdrop; if it describes something that happens for the first time, render it

## What NOT to Do

- Do not open with establishing-shot descriptions of the setting
- Do not write exposition paragraphs — scatter worldbuilding through experience
- Do not label emotions as the primary rendering ("he felt grief," "anger rose in him"). Emotions must be rendered physically first. Two exceptions: **negation** (name the emotion to deny it, then render physically: "Not relief — her hands were still locked on the housing") and **retrospective labeling** (name the emotion after the physical rendering, as analytical categorization: "Later she'd call it grief. At the time it was just the pressure behind her eyes").
- Do not have characters state their feelings in dialogue
- Do not wrap up the scene with a thematic summary
- Do not use filter words — "he realized," "he noticed," "he felt," "he saw," "he heard" — unless the act of perceiving IS the dramatic action, not a filter on it. "She knew what she was" (the knowing is the point) is permitted. "She noticed the hatch" (noticing filters the real event) is not.
- Do not use vocabulary that violates the project's setting, genre, or style guide
- Do not use similes unless they deliver information the literal description cannot reach (within the Style Guide's stated appetite — some voices are figurative by design, and the Style Guide's decision wins)
- Do not tell what you've already shown (if the physical rendering needs a label, strengthen the rendering)
- Do not use AI-favored constructions, words, formulaic sentence patterns, punctuation habits, or structural tics — the patterns and density thresholds live in `.proselon/workflow/AI Prose Tendencies.md`, starting with its Core Tells (Drafting Checklist) section. Avoid them while drafting rather than relying on the Line Assessment to catch them.

## Candidate Drafting (for the orchestrating agent)

The first draft is the highest-variance generation in the pipeline, and the only place the scene's *content* is invented: the actual dialogue lines, beat choices, images, and entry point are created here and survive every later pass — the Voice Rewrite changes how things sound, never what they mean. A draft whose invention is merely adequate caps the finished scene at adequate. So don't take one sample:

- Generate **2–3 independent drafts** in separate fresh contexts, each following this doc in full, none seeing another. Use different strong models where available — two contexts of the same model share invention habits as well as tics. **Candidates never touch the manuscript file:** each drafting context returns its draft as output (or writes to a scratch location outside `Manuscripts/`); only the winner is written to the manuscript path, by the orchestrating agent, after judging.
- A fresh craft-tier **judge** — loading the scene plot, the Model Passages corpus, and the candidates labeled only A/B/C (no model names, no context identifiers — that's what blind means here) — picks the strongest **dramatization**: the liveliest dialogue, the most specific imagery, the best-rendered beats, the strongest entry point. A short paragraph of reasoning goes in the scene report.
- **Pick, never merge.** Stitching passages from different candidates produces prose with no single rhythm. The judge may note a standout moment in a losing candidate for the Writer's awareness in later passes, but the winning draft proceeds whole.

The winner is written to the manuscript file and enters the pipeline as *the* draft. Discard the others (a genuinely remarkable stray passage may be parked in `Fragments/`).

## The Goal

Get the shape of the scene down. Hit the emotional arc from the scene plot. Land the key dialogue beats. Don't over-polish — that's what the editing passes are for. A rough but honest draft that captures the scene's spine is better than a polished draft that misses its heart.

Estimated scene and chapter word counts are planning tools meant to keep the project on track as a whole. Individual scenes or chapters may go under or over their estimates when the material needs it; flag meaningful deviations for review, but do not cut or pad prose only to satisfy the estimate.

Write the scene directly in the manuscript file — unless you are drafting as one of several candidates, in which case return the draft instead and the orchestrating agent writes the winner (see Candidate Drafting). Keep scene plots and planning notes out of exportable manuscript files.
