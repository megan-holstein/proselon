# Style and Voice Assessment

Evaluate the Style Guide and Voice Reference as a set. This assessment verifies that prose decisions are traceable to genre conventions, that every subversion is justified, and that the voice is distinctive enough to guide drafting.

## Prerequisites

Read these files before assessing:

1. `Plot/Specs.md` — especially the Prose Style Conventions section *(if present)*
2. `Plot/Series Plot.md` — for series-level tone and themes *(if present)*
3. `Style/Style Guide.md` — the style guide being assessed
4. `Style/Voice Reference.md` — the voice reference being assessed

Assess against what exists at this point in the workflow. A missing file marked *(if present)* is not a weakness; skip the criteria that depend on it.

## Checklist

### Genre-Convention Coverage

Every prose dimension in the style guide references the corresponding genre convention from `Plot/Specs.md`. No conventions are ignored without acknowledgment. If the genre research documented a convention, the style guide addresses it — either following or subverting.

### Subversion Justification

Every departure from a genre convention is connected to plot, theme, or character. The Intentional Subversions Summary table is complete and every entry has a specific rationale — not "because it works better" but a concrete story reason.

### Convention Adherence

Where the style guide follows genre conventions, the stated rules would produce prose that meets reader expectations for the genre. Following a convention should be an active decision, not an empty acknowledgment.

### Voice Specificity

The voice reference describes a distinctive narrative consciousness — not generic good writing. It has identifiable habits, preferences, and blind spots. A reader could distinguish this voice from other competent fiction in the same genre.

### Thematic Connection

The style and voice reinforce themes through prose choices. The voice's attitudes, rhythms, and attention patterns connect to the story's thematic material — not by being "about" the themes, but by embodying them in how the prose moves.

### Internal Consistency

The style guide and voice reference do not contradict each other. The voice reference's personality operates within the style guide's rules. Where they describe overlapping territory (e.g., sentence rhythm, emotional rendering), they agree.

### Actionability

Both documents are specific enough to follow. The style guide gives concrete rules, not just intentions. The voice reference provides patterns and examples, not just descriptions. A drafting agent could read both documents and produce prose that matches — without needing to guess what the author meant.

### Model Passage Quality

The voice reference's model passages demonstrate the claimed distinctive features. The passages are not generic good writing with the distinctive features described but not shown. The anti-model passages clearly illustrate what the voice sounds like when it goes flat.

The corpus respects the three-tier priority structure: Author-Selected passages outrank Borrowed (a provisional scaffold from admired books), which outrank Agent-Selected, and the three together stay within the 1,500–3,000-word budget. Empty lower tiers are expected and correct as the author's own prose fills the budget — not gaps. At project start, before the author has prose of their own, a corpus built only from a light borrowed scaffold and/or agent samples is acceptable — provided the author was actually asked for their own prose first, and any borrowed scaffold stays small and varied rather than cloning one writer.

### Dialogue Coverage

The corpus includes dialogue-heavy passages labeled with their speakers — building toward at least one per major speaking character as the corpus matures (a pure-narration corpus is a weakness to flag, not a blocker at project start). Each Other Character Voices entry demonstrates as well as describes: signature lines specific enough that a drafter could continue them, and a would-never-say line. Voices that read as interchangeable — descriptions that would fit any character — fail this check.

### Red Flag Completeness

The style guide's Red Flag List is derived from genre conventions and voice boundaries. It covers AI-prose tendencies that would undermine the project's specific voice — not just a generic list of bad writing patterns.

## Output Format

```
## Style and Voice Assessment

**Strengths** (ranked)
1. ...

**Weaknesses** (ranked by severity)
1. ...

**Required Revisions**
- [Only include if verdict is Needs revision. Each item should be specific enough to edit the style guide or voice reference.]

**Verdict**: Approved / Needs revision
```

Concise, ranked, actionable. If the verdict is Needs revision, weaknesses must include specific enough detail to act on.

Use Needs revision only for issues that would mislead drafting — rules too vague to follow, a voice indistinguishable from generic competent prose, contradictions between the two documents, or unjustified departures from genre conventions. Do not withhold approval because the corpus is still small or the voice will keep evolving; it converges as the author's prose accumulates.
