# Voice Reference Template

Use this template to create a project voice reference alongside the style guide. The voice reference captures the specific narrative consciousness — not rules (that's the Style Guide) but personality. What the prose sounds like when it's working.

## Interview Goals

Before filling the template, gather enough information to answer:

- How does the POV character see the world? What do they notice first, what do they ignore, what do they misread?
- What does the voice sound like at its best — what makes it distinctive rather than generically competent?
- What does the AI version of this voice sound like — the version we want to avoid?
- How do different characters talk? What speech patterns, vocabularies, rhythms distinguish them?
- What is the narrator's relationship to the material — wry, earnest, detached, intimate?

Do not draft manuscript prose during the interview unless the author explicitly asks.

## Context to Load

Read these files before beginning the interview:

1. `Plot/Specs.md` — for genre voice expectations
2. `Plot/Series Plot.md` — for series-level tone
3. Any character profiles in `Worldbuilding/Characters/` — for POV character's psychology, background, and worldview (if any have been created)
4. `Style/Style Guide.md` — for the prose rules the voice must operate within
5. Any existing `Style/Voice Reference.md` — if revising rather than creating

## Model Passages

An exemplar corpus of **1,500–3,000 words** of target-voice prose, covering the same registers as Rhythm Patterns (calm/observational, under threat, intimate/vulnerable, action/urgency). This corpus is the single highest-leverage part of the style system: models are mediocre at *following described* style and excellent at *continuing demonstrated* style. The Style Guide is the rulebook; this corpus is the demonstration.

The corpus has three tiers, in strict priority order. All three share the one word budget; when space is tight, fill from the top and cut from the bottom. Label every passage with the register and aspect it demonstrates. (See "Building the Corpus" below for how to gather and balance them.)

1. **Author-Selected Passages** — the author's own prose, hand-picked. The real target; preferred above all, never trimmed without the author's say-so.
2. **Borrowed Passages** — short samples from books the author loves, used only as a provisional cold-start scaffold. Retired as the author's own prose accumulates.
3. **Agent-Selected Passages** — agent-written samples, the weakest tier; fills only registers the tiers above leave uncovered.

## Building the Corpus

The Model Passages corpus shapes drafted prose more than anything else in Steps 1–4. Don't let it fill with agent-written samples by default. Work the tiers in priority order:

1. **Ask for the author's own prose first.** If they've drafted scenes they're happy with, invite them to point you at their favorite passages — the parts that already sound like the book in their head. This is the strongest material there is:

   > "If you've written any scenes you're happy with, point me at your favorite passages — the parts that sound most like the book in your head. Prose from your own story shapes every page I draft more than any rule I could write, so it always comes first here."

   Gather as much as the author wants to give. These go under Author-Selected Passages and are never trimmed without their say-so.

2. **No prose yet? Offer a borrowed scaffold — clearly temporary.** Early on, an empty corpus is the costly case: with nothing demonstrated, the first scenes draft against no target and come out generic, and those first scenes set the attractor the book converges toward (see Voice Bootstrapping). So seed a light scaffold rather than leaving it empty — but mark it as scaffolding and keep it small and varied, so drafting takes direction without imitating one writer:

   > "No prose yet is normal this early — your best passages will come from your own scenes as you write them. For a head start you can point me at a few short bits from books you love; I'll treat them as temporary scaffolding and swap them out for your own prose the moment you've written scenes that sound right. Or I can seed a light set myself."

   Borrowed passages the author names go under Borrowed Passages — keep them to a handful across registers, ideally from more than one author.

3. **Fill remaining registers with agent passages, last.** Only after the tiers above, and only for registers still uncovered, write samples calibrated to the described voice (Narrative Consciousness, Rhythm Patterns). These go under Agent-Selected Passages. If the tiers above already fill the budget, add none.

4. **Hold the budget; protect the author's tier.** The three tiers share one 1,500–3,000-word budget. When it's tight, cut from the bottom up — Agent first, then Borrowed — never the author's own. If the author's *own* selections exceed 3,000 words, don't silently truncate; tell them they're past the useful ceiling and offer to help trim, but let them decide what goes:

   > "You've given me about [N] words, past the ~3,000 that stays useful — beyond that the signal dilutes. Want me to flag the few I think are pulling least weight? You decide what stays."

## Voice Bootstrapping

Before the author has prose of their own, the corpus leans on a borrowed scaffold and agent samples. Bootstrap toward the author's real voice deliberately rather than letting it happen by accident:

1. **The first ~3 scenes get disproportionate editing attention** — this is where the author's heaviest line-by-line involvement belongs. Invite it. Voice sign-off on these scenes is a hard blocker even in autopilot: get explicit approval before drafting further.
2. **When the author declares a scene "sounds right," promote its best passages into Author-Selected Passages** (now author-validated prose), labeled by register — and retire scaffold to stay in budget: drop Agent-Selected first, then Borrowed.
3. Because adjacent scenes are drafting context, voice compounds: whatever the early chapters sound like, the book converges toward. The first few scenes set the attractor — bootstrap quality on purpose.

These rules also ship inside the output template's Corpus Maintenance section, so the saved `Style/Voice Reference.md` carries its own upkeep into every drafting session — drafting sessions load the artifact, not this template. Keep that section when filling the template.

## Completion Criteria

A usable voice reference:

- Describes a distinctive narrative consciousness — not generic good writing, but a specific sensibility with identifiable habits, preferences, and blind spots.
- Includes a Model Passages corpus that demonstrates the claimed distinctive features in practice across the major registers — building toward 1,500–3,000 words as the project matures.
- Was built by inviting the author's own prose first; author-selected passages outrank borrowed (scaffold) and agent passages, and the three tiers together stay within the word budget.
- Includes anti-model passages that show the generic version to avoid.
- Is specific enough that two different agents given this reference would produce similar-sounding prose.
- Differentiates major characters' dialogue voices from each other and from the narrator.
- Does not contradict the Style Guide (`Style/Style Guide.md`).
