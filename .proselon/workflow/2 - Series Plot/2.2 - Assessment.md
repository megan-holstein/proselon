# Series Plot Assessment

Evaluate the series-level shape enough to support the book currently being developed. This is the first tier of plot assessment — clear the series plot before drilling into the active book.

The whole series does not need to be plotted in sequential detail before work continues. Only the active book needs enough ordered structure to support book-level assessment. Later books need a clear function, continuity commitments, required coverage, and known open questions so the active book does not contradict or strand future material.

## Prerequisites

Read these files before assessing:

1. `Plot/Series Plot.md` — series arc, active-book context, and later-book functions
2. `Plot/Themes and Conflict.md` — thematic commitments *(if it exists — it is Step 3's output and won't exist on first assessment)*
3. `Plot/Specs.md` — word count targets, pacing guidelines, series promises
4. Project character profiles *(those that exist)*
5. Project worldbuilding, setting, history, and continuity references *(those that exist)*

Assess against what exists at this point in the workflow. A missing upstream file from a later step is not a weakness; skip the criteria that depend on it.

## Procedure

Work through every criterion below. The checklist is your internal rubric — only findings appear in the output. For each criterion, determine whether it holds, partially holds, or fails at the level required for current development.

Assess future books for continuity and coverage, not for full sequential plotting. A later book should not receive a weakness merely because its chapter-by-chapter or scene-by-scene path is still open.

### Checklist

- **Series promises tracked**: Each promise in `Plot/Specs.md` has a delivery area or planned payoff. No promise is introduced and forgotten. Future-book delivery points may remain broad.
- **Active-book support**: The series shape gives the active book enough context to make its ending, unresolved threads, and next-book handoff meaningful.
- **Later-book coverage**: Each later book has a clear function and states what major material it must cover before the following book or series end. Later books do not need full sequential plot.
- **Escalation pattern**: Stakes rise book to book. Each book's central conflict is more demanding than the last, even if future-book mechanics are still broad.
- **Tonal arc variation**: Each book has a distinct emotional register and function in the larger series. The series does not feel monotone.
- **Thread continuity**: Major threads (character arcs, relationship dynamics, external conflicts, mysteries, promises, social pressures, or plot engines) carry across book boundaries without gaps or contradictions. Future-book threads may be tracked as required developments or open questions.
- **Character arc coverage**: Every major character relevant to the active book has a clear current role and carry-forward state. Future resolution may remain broad if it is named and not contradicted.
- **Thematic payoff** *(only when `Plot/Themes and Conflict.md` exists — i.e., on re-assessment after Step 3)*: Each theme in `Plot/Themes and Conflict.md` is explored progressively and has a planned payoff, reframing, or carry-forward destination. Future details may remain open. On first assessment, skip this criterion.
- **Genre or story-system consistency**: Any magic, technology, politics, law, profession, institution, relationship rule, mystery logic, social system, or other governing rules hold throughout. Exceptions needed for the active book are foreshadowed. Future capability escalation must not break the system as currently defined.
- **Core relationship or emotional engine**: The central relationship, rivalry, community, or inner conflict deepens, fractures, and resolves across the series.
- **Promise handling**: The series' central promises, questions, mysteries, or expectations stay active until their payoff. Multiple outcomes or interpretations remain plausible when appropriate. Promises create tension rather than certainty.
- **Worldbuilding-to-plot ratio**: Worldbuilding serves plot and character, not the reverse. The world's rules create the plot's obstacles — if an obstacle could exist in any setting, the worldbuilding is decorative.

## Output Format

```
## Series Plot Assessment

**Strengths** (ranked)
1. ...

**Weaknesses** (ranked by severity)
1. ...

**Verdict**: Approved / Needs revision
```

Concise, ranked, actionable. If the verdict is Needs revision, weaknesses must include specific enough detail to act on.

Use Needs revision only for issues that block or endanger the active book's development, create continuity contradictions, abandon promised material, or make later books unable to cover what they must cover. Do not require a complete plot for later books before assessing the active book.
